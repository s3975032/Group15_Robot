Testing

In the project, there will be a mandatory usability test in a lab setting. The pre-release development stage is considered successful after all the errors and pain points discovered in the testing session are fully fixed. This test will be conducted as a task within the scope of this project, not for validation purposes but for the involvement of the users early in the project. Based on our experience with a UX designer on the team, this approach is related to user-centred design methodology, which is the best way to design an interactive product.

Based on a study conducted by Jakob Nielsen (2000), only 5 testing subjects are needed to identify 85% of the usability problems. Given that we plan to include usability testing in the project, the data collection needs to be fast, concise, and non-cluttered; thus, having around 5 subjects is the best return-on-investment choice we can make. We are aware that not all errors will be discovered, but the most pressing ones will undoubtedly be revealed with just 5 test subjects. More minor errors can be debugged using user feedback when the projectâ€™s products are in the hands of the public.

Hitting the snooze button on an alarm clock and struggling to get up in the morning is a common struggle, so finding 5 relevant subjects should not be a challenging endeavour. Subjects just need to fit one of the following background requirements:

  • Night owls who struggle to get up in the morning
  • People whose job requires them to wake up at weird hours
  • The "serial snoozers" who press that snooze button to get an extra 5 minutes of sleep at least 2 times every morning
